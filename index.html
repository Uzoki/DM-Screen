    Conditions
    Blinded
    New Rules (2024)
    • You can't see and automatically fail any ability check that requires sight.
    • Attack rolls against you have Advantage, and your attack rolls have Disadvantage.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A blinded creature can't see and automatically fails any ability check that requires sight.
    • Attack rolls against the creature have advantage, and the creature's attack rolls have disadvantage.
    Charmed
    New Rules (2024)
    • You can't attack the charmer or target the charmer with damaging abilities or magical effects.
    • The charmer has Advantage on any ability check to interact with you socially.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A charmed creature can't attack the charmer or target the charmer with harmful abilities or magical effects.
    • The charmer has advantage on any ability check to interact socially with the creature.
    Deafened
    New Rules (2024)
    • You can't hear and automatically fail any ability check that requires hearing.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A deafened creature can't hear and automatically fails any ability check that requires hearing.
    Exhaustion
    New Rules (2024)
    • Exhaustion is cumulative and measured in levels, from 1 to 6.
    • Each level gives you a cumulative −2 penalty to every d20 roll (ability checks, attack rolls, and saving throws).
    • Each level also reduces your Speed by 5 feet, cumulative.
    • If your Exhaustion level reaches 6, you die.
    • Finishing a long rest removes 1 Exhaustion level.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • Exhaustion is cumulative and measured in levels, from 1 to 6, with effects that stack as the level increases.
    • Level 1: Disadvantage on ability checks.
    • Level 2: Speed halved.
    • Level 3: Disadvantage on attack rolls and saving throws.
    • Level 4: Hit point maximum halved.
    • Level 5: Speed reduced to 0.
    • Level 6: Death.
    • Finishing a long rest reduces Exhaustion by 1 level.

    Frightened
    New Rules (2024)
    • You have Disadvantage on ability checks and attack rolls while the source of fear is within line of sight.
    • You can't willingly move closer to the source of fear.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A frightened creature has disadvantage on ability checks and attack rolls while the source of its fear is within line of sight.
    • The creature can't willingly move closer to the source of its fear.
    Grappled
    New Rules (2024)
    • Your Speed is 0 and can't increase.
    • You have Disadvantage on attack rolls against any target other than the grappler.
    • The grappler can drag or carry you when it moves, but every foot of movement costs it 1 extra foot unless you are Tiny or two or more sizes smaller than it.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A grappled creature's speed becomes 0, and it can't benefit from any bonus to its speed.
    • The condition ends if the grappler is incapacitated (see the condition).
    • The condition also ends if an effect removes the grappled creature from the reach of the grappler or grappling effect, such as when a creature is hurled away by the thunderwave spell.
    Incapacitated
    New Rules (2024)
    • You can't take any action, Bonus Action, or Reaction.
    • Your Concentration is broken.
    • You can't speak.
    • If you're Incapacitated when you roll Initiative, you have Disadvantage on the roll.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • An incapacitated creature can't take actions or reactions.
    Invisible
    New Rules (2024)
    • If you're Invisible when you roll Initiative, you have Advantage on the roll.
    • You aren't affected by any effect that requires its target to be seen, unless the effect's creator can somehow see you. Any equipment you're wearing or carrying is also concealed.
    • Attack rolls against you have Disadvantage, and your attack rolls have Advantage, unless the attacker can somehow see you.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • An invisible creature is impossible to see without the aid of magic or a special sense. For the purpose of hiding, the creature is heavily obscured. The creature's location can be detected by any noise it makes or any tracks it leaves.
    • Attack rolls against the creature have disadvantage, and the creature's attack rolls have advantage.
    Paralyzed
    New Rules (2024)
    • You have the Incapacitated condition.
    • Your Speed is 0 and can't increase.
    • You automatically fail Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • Attack rolls against you have Advantage.
    • Any attack roll that hits you is a Critical Hit if the attacker is within 5 feet of you.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A paralyzed creature is incapacitated (see the condition) and can't move or speak.
    • The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • Attack rolls against the creature have advantage.
    • Any attack that hits the creature is a critical hit if the attacker is within 5 feet of the creature.
    Petrified
    New Rules (2024)
    • You're transformed, along with any nonmagical objects you're wearing and carrying, into a solid inanimate substance (usually stone). Your weight increases by a factor of ten, and you cease aging.
    • You have the Incapacitated condition.
    • Your Speed is 0 and can't increase.
    • Attack rolls against you have Advantage.
    • You automatically fail Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • You have Resistance to all damage.
    • You have Immunity to the Poisoned condition (note: as of 2024 you are no longer immune to disease while petrified).
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A petrified creature is transformed, along with any nonmagical object it is wearing or carrying, into a solid inanimate substance (usually stone). Its weight increases by a factor of ten, and it ceases aging.
    • The creature is incapacitated (see the condition), can't move or speak, and is unaware of its surroundings.
    • Attack rolls against the creature have advantage.
    • The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • The creature has resistance to all damage.
    • The creature is immune to poison and disease, although a poison or disease already in its system is suspended, not neutralized.
    Poisoned
    New Rules (2024)
    • You have Disadvantage on attack rolls and ability checks.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A poisoned creature has disadvantage on attack rolls and ability checks.
    Prone
    New Rules (2024)
    • Your only movement options are to crawl, or to spend an amount of movement equal to half your Speed (round down) to right yourself and end the condition. If your Speed is 0, you can't right yourself.
    • You have Disadvantage on attack rolls. An attack roll against you has Advantage if the attacker is within 5 feet of you. Otherwise, that attack roll has Disadvantage.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A prone creature's only movement option is to crawl, unless it stands up and thereby ends the condition.
    • The creature has disadvantage on attack rolls.
    • An attack roll against the creature has advantage if the attacker is within 5 feet of the creature. Otherwise, the attack roll has disadvantage.
    Restrained
    New Rules (2024)
    • Your Speed is 0 and can't increase.
    • Attack rolls against you have Advantage, and your attack rolls have Disadvantage.
    • You have Disadvantage on Dexterity saving throws.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A restrained creature's speed becomes 0, and it can't benefit from any bonus to its speed.
    • Attack rolls against the creature have advantage, and the creature's attack rolls have disadvantage.
    • The creature has disadvantage on Dexterity saving throws.
    Stunned
    New Rules (2024)
    • You have the Incapacitated condition.
    • You automatically fail Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • Attack rolls against you have Advantage.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • A stunned creature is incapacitated (see the condition), can't move, and can speak only falteringly.
    • The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • Attack rolls against the creature have advantage.
    Unconscious
    New Rules (2024)
    • You have the Incapacitated and Prone conditions, and you drop whatever you're holding. When this condition ends, you remain Prone.
    • Your Speed is 0 and can't increase.
    • Attack rolls against you have Advantage.
    • You automatically fail Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • Any attack roll that hits you is a Critical Hit if the attacker is within 5 feet of you.
    • You're unaware of your surroundings.
    Legacy Rules (2014)
    • An unconscious creature is incapacitated, can't move or speak, and is unaware of its surroundings.
    • The creature drops whatever it's holding and falls prone.
    • The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws.
    • Attack rolls against the creature have advantage.

    Ability Check

    A D20 Test that represents using one of the six abilities — or a specific skill associated with an ability — to overcome a challenge.

    Ability Score and Modifier

    A creature has six ability scores — Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma — each with a corresponding modifier. Add the modifier when making a D20 Test with that ability, or whenever a rule says to.

    Acrobatics

    A Dexterity skill covering staying on your feet in a tricky situation, such as running across ice, balancing on a tightrope, or staying upright on a rocking ship's deck. It's also the check you make to perform acrobatic stunts, including dives, rolls, somersaults, and flips.

    Action

    On your turn you can take one action. The special named actions (Attack, Dash, Disengage, Dodge, Help, Hide, Influence, Magic, Ready, Search, Study, Utilize) each have their own entry below, in addition to whatever special actions your features provide.

    Advantage

    If you have Advantage on a D20 Test, roll two d20s and use the higher roll. A roll can't be affected by more than one Advantage, and Advantage and Disadvantage on the same roll cancel each other out.

    Adventure

    A series of encounters. A story emerges through playing them.

    Alignment

    A creature's alignment broadly describes its ethical attitudes and ideals: one factor identifies morality (good, evil, or neutral), the other describes attitude toward order (lawful, chaotic, or neutral) — nine possible combinations in total, such as Lawful Good or Neutral Evil.

    Ally

    A creature is your ally if it's a member of your adventuring party, your friend, on your side in combat, or designated as your ally by the rules or the DM.

    Animal Handling

    A Wisdom skill used to calm a domesticated animal, keep a mount from bolting, or intuit an animal's intentions. The DM might also call for this check to control your mount when you attempt a risky maneuver.

    Arcana

    An Intelligence skill measuring your ability to recall lore about spells, magic items, eldritch symbols, magical traditions, the planes of existence, and the inhabitants of those planes. See also the Creature Types by Skill dropdown above.

    Area of Effect

    Many spells and features have an area of effect, typically one of six shapes: Cone, Cube, Cylinder, Emanation, Line, or Sphere (each has its own entry below).

    An area of effect has a point of origin, from which its energy erupts. If every straight line from the point of origin to a location in the area is blocked by an obstruction giving Total Cover, that location isn't included. If the origin point is placed unseen behind an obstruction such as a wall, the origin instead forms on the near side of it.

    Armor Class

    The target number for an attack roll — how difficult a target is to hit. Base AC is 10 plus your Dexterity modifier, unless a rule gives you another base AC calculation; you can only use one such calculation at a time.

    Armor Training

    Lets you use armor of a certain category without penalty. Wearing Light, Medium, or Heavy armor without training gives Disadvantage on any Strength or Dexterity D20 Test and prevents casting spells. Using a Shield without training means you don't gain its AC bonus.

    Athletics

    A Strength skill covering difficult situations while climbing, jumping, or swimming, such as pulling yourself over a ledge, jumping farther than normal, or avoiding hazards in rough water. It's also the check used to grapple or shove a creature in melee.

    Attack [Action]

    Lets you make one attack roll with a weapon or an Unarmed Strike.

    • Equipping/Unequipping. You can equip or unequip one weapon as part of this action, before or after the attack, without needing to use it for that attack.
    • Moving Between Attacks. If a feature such as Extra Attack gives you more than one attack, you can split your movement between those attacks.
    Attack Roll

    A D20 Test representing an attack with a weapon, an Unarmed Strike, or a spell.

    Attitude

    A monster's starting attitude toward a player character: Friendly, Hostile, or Indifferent.

    Attunement

    Some magic items require forming a bond — Attunement — before their magical properties can be used. A creature can be attuned to no more than three magic items at a time.

    Blindsight

    Lets you see within a set range without relying on physical sight. You can see anything not behind Total Cover in that range even if Blinded or in Darkness, and you can see creatures with the Invisible condition within that range.

    Bloodied

    A creature is Bloodied while it has half its Hit Points or fewer remaining.

    Bonus Action

    A special action you can take on the same turn as your action. You can't take more than one Bonus Action per turn, and only when a rule explicitly grants one.

    Bright Light

    Normal illumination.

    Burning [Hazard]

    A burning creature or object takes 1d4 Fire damage at the start of each of its turns. As an action, a creature can extinguish itself by dropping Prone and rolling on the ground. Fire also goes out if doused, submerged, or suffocated.

    Burrow Speed

    Lets a creature move through sand, earth, mud, or ice. It can't burrow through solid rock unless a trait says otherwise.

    Campaign

    A series of adventures.

    Cantrip

    A level 0 spell, cast without using a spell slot.

    Challenge Rating

    Summarizes the threat a monster poses to four player characters. Higher than party level suggests danger, lower suggests little threat — but circumstances and party size can change this significantly in play.

    Character Sheet

    A paper or digital record used to track your character's information.

    Charisma

    One of the six ability scores. Charisma measures your ability to influence, entertain, or deceive others through force of personality. It governs the Deception, Intimidation, Performance, and Persuasion skills, and is the spellcasting ability for Bards, Paladins, Sorcerers, and Warlocks.

    Climbing

    Each foot of movement while climbing costs 1 extra foot (2 extra in Difficult Terrain) unless you have and use a Climb Speed. The DM may require a DC 15 Strength (Athletics) check on a slippery surface or one with few handholds.

    Climb Speed

    Used in place of Speed to climb a vertical surface without the extra movement cost normally required.

    Concentration

    Some spells and effects require Concentration to stay active. It can be ended voluntarily at any time (no action). It's broken by any of the following:

    • Another Concentration Effect. Starting to cast a Concentration spell, or activating another Concentration effect, ends the previous one.
    • Damage. Taking damage requires a Constitution save (DC = 10 or half the damage taken, whichever is higher, max DC 30) to maintain it.
    • Incapacitated or Dead. Concentration ends immediately if you're Incapacitated or die.
    Condition

    A temporary game state that affects its recipient in a defined way. A condition doesn't stack with itself — a creature either has it or doesn't — except Exhaustion, which is cumulative. See the Conditions dropdown above for the full list.

    Cone [Area of Effect]

    Extends in straight lines from a point of origin in a chosen direction. Its width at any point equals that point's distance from the origin (e.g. 15 feet wide at 15 feet from the origin). The origin itself isn't included in the area unless its creator says otherwise.

    Crawling

    Each foot of movement while crawling costs 1 extra foot (2 extra in Difficult Terrain).

    Creature

    Any being in the game, including a player character, is a creature.

    Creature Type

    Every creature has a tag identifying its type (most player characters are Humanoid). Types have no rules of their own, but some rules affect certain types differently. See the Creature Types by Skill dropdown above.

    Critical Hit

    Rolling a natural 20 on an attack roll scores a Critical Hit, which always hits regardless of AC or modifiers. Roll all the attack's damage dice twice and add them together, then add relevant modifiers.

    Cube [Area of Effect]

    Extends in straight lines from a point of origin located anywhere on one of its faces. The effect that creates it specifies the length of each side. The origin isn't included in the area unless its creator says otherwise.

    Curses

    The effect that confers a curse defines what it does. Curses can be removed by Remove Curse, Greater Restoration, or other magic that explicitly ends curses.

    Cylinder [Area of Effect]

    Extends in straight lines from a point of origin at the center of the circular top or bottom face. The effect specifies the base radius and the height. Unlike most shapes, the origin point IS included in the area.

    D20 Test

    Covers the three main d20 rolls: ability checks, attack rolls, and saving throws. Anything affecting D20 Tests affects all three. The DM decides when a D20 Test is warranted.

    Damage

    Represents harm that causes a creature or object to lose Hit Points.

    Damage Roll

    A die roll, adjusted by applicable modifiers, that deals damage to a target.

    Damage Threshold

    A creature or object with a damage threshold has Immunity to all damage unless a single attack or effect deals damage equal to or greater than the threshold, in which case it takes the full amount. Damage that fails to meet the threshold is superficial and does nothing.

    Damage Types

    Acid, Bludgeoning, Cold, Fire, Force, Lightning, Necrotic, Piercing, Poison, Psychic, Radiant, Slashing, Thunder. Damage types carry no rules of their own, but other rules (like Resistance) key off them.

    Darkness

    An area of Darkness is Heavily Obscured.

    Darkvision

    Lets you see in Dim Light within a set range as if it were Bright Light, and in Darkness within that range as if it were Dim Light (colors appear only as shades of gray in that Darkness).

    Dash [Action]

    Gives extra movement equal to your Speed (after modifiers) for the current turn. You may use a special speed (like Fly Speed) instead of your normal Speed, chosen each time you Dash.

    Dead

    A dead creature has no Hit Points and can't regain them without magic like Raise Dead or Revivify — the spirit must consent, having departed for the Outer Planes. On revival it keeps ongoing conditions, contagions, or curses (if durations haven't lapsed), returns with 1 fewer Exhaustion level if it died with any, and loses any Attunement it had.

    Death Saving Throw

    A player character must make a Death Save if they start their turn at 0 Hit Points.

    Deception

    A Charisma skill used to convincingly hide the truth, whether verbally or through your actions — lying, fast-talking a guard, or wearing a disguise.

    Dehydration [Hazard]

    Drinking less than half a day's required water gives 1 Exhaustion level at day's end, which can't be removed until a full day's water is drunk.

    Dexterity

    One of the six ability scores. Dexterity measures agility, reflexes, and balance. It governs your base Armor Class, Initiative, and the Acrobatics, Sleight of Hand, and Stealth skills, along with ranged and Finesse weapon attacks.

    Difficult Terrain

    Every foot of movement in Difficult Terrain costs 1 extra foot (not cumulative — a space either is or isn't Difficult Terrain). A space qualifies if it contains, among other things: a non-Tiny non-ally creature, furniture sized for your size or larger, heavy snow/ice/rubble/undergrowth, liquid shin-to-waist deep, a narrow opening one size smaller than you, or a slope of 20 degrees or more.

    Difficulty Class

    The target number (DC) for an ability check or saving throw.

    Dim Light

    An area of Dim Light is Lightly Obscured.

    Disadvantage

    If you have Disadvantage on a D20 Test, roll two d20s and use the lower roll. A roll can't be affected by more than one Disadvantage, and Advantage/Disadvantage on the same roll cancel out.

    Disengage [Action]

    Your movement doesn't provoke Opportunity Attacks for the rest of the turn.

    Dodge [Action]

    Until the start of your next turn, attack rolls against you have Disadvantage if you can see the attacker, and you make Dexterity saves with Advantage. Lost if you're Incapacitated or your Speed is 0.

    Emanation [Area of Effect]

    Extends in straight lines from a creature or object in all directions, out to a distance the effect specifies. It moves with its origin unless instantaneous or stationary. The origin itself isn't included in the area unless its creator says otherwise.

    Encounter

    A scene in an adventure belonging to at least one of the game's three pillars: social interaction, exploration, or combat.

    Enemy

    A creature is your enemy if it fights against you, actively works to harm you, or is designated your enemy by the rules or DM.

    Experience Points

    Awarded by the DM for overcoming challenges and completing adventures. Crossing certain XP thresholds increases a character's level.

    Expertise

    Doubles your Proficiency Bonus on an ability check using a skill proficiency you have Expertise in (unless already doubled by another feature). Expertise is gained in a skill you're already proficient in, and can't stack twice in the same skill.

    Falling [Hazard]

    A fall deals 1d6 Bludgeoning damage at the end of the fall per 10 feet fallen, max 20d6, and leaves you Prone on landing unless you take no damage. Falling into liquid lets you use your Reaction for a DC 15 Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics) check to hit head/feet first, halving the damage on a success.

    Flying

    While flying, you fall if Incapacitated, Prone, or your Fly Speed drops to 0 — unless you can hover.

    Fly Speed

    Lets you travel through the air. You can stay aloft until you land, fall, or die.

    Friendly [Attitude]

    A Friendly creature views you favorably; you have Advantage on ability checks to influence it.

    Grappling

    Characters typically grapple with an Unarmed Strike; some monsters have special grapple attacks.

    • Grappled Condition. A successful grapple gives the target the Grappled condition.
    • One Grapple per Hand. A free hand (or equivalent body part) is needed, and it can grapple only one creature at a time.
    • Ending a Grapple. The target can use its action for a Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics) check against the escape DC. It also ends if the grappler is Incapacitated, if the target is moved beyond the grapple's range, or if the grappler releases it (no action required).
    Hazard

    An environmental danger — see Burning, Dehydration, Falling, Malnutrition, and Suffocation.

    Healing

    How you regain Hit Points.

    Heavily Obscured

    You have the Blinded condition while trying to see something in a Heavily Obscured space.

    Help [Action]
    • Assist an Ability Check. Choose a skill/tool proficiency and a nearby ally; they get Advantage on the next check with that skill/tool before the start of your next turn. DM has final say on feasibility.
    • Assist an Attack Roll. Distract an enemy within 5 feet, giving an ally Advantage on their next attack against it before the start of your next turn.
    Heroic Inspiration

    Can be expended to reroll any die immediately after rolling it (must use the new result). Gaining it while you already have it is lost unless given to a player character who lacks it.

    Hide [Action]

    Requires a successful DC 15 Dexterity (Stealth) check while Heavily Obscured or behind Three-Quarters/Total Cover, and out of any enemy's line of sight. On success, you have the Invisible condition while hidden; the check total becomes the DC for others to find you via Wisdom (Perception).

    Hiding ends immediately if you make a sound louder than a whisper, an enemy finds you, you make an attack roll, or you cast a spell with a Verbal component.

    High Jump

    Jump vertically 3 + Strength modifier feet (min 0) with a 10-foot running start; half that standing still. Each foot costs a foot of movement. You can reach half your height higher during the jump, for a total reach of the jump height plus 1.5x your height.

    History

    An Intelligence skill measuring your ability to recall lore about historical events, legendary people, ancient kingdoms, past disputes, and lost civilizations.

    Hit Point Dice

    Help determine a character's HP maximum. Most monsters have them too. Can be spent during a Short Rest to regain HP.

    Hit Points

    A measure of how hard a creature or object is to kill or destroy. Damage reduces HP, healing restores it, and it can never exceed the maximum or drop below 0.

    Hostile [Attitude]

    A Hostile creature views you unfavorably; you have Disadvantage on ability checks to influence it.

    Hover

    Some creatures, spells, or effects grant the ability to hover, which prevents falling while flying in certain circumstances.

    Illusions

    The effect that creates an illusion defines what it does and which senses it deceives. An illusion is insubstantial and weightless but seems affected by its environment (casting shadows, echoing, etc.) unless stated otherwise.

    Immunity

    If you have Immunity to a damage type or condition, it doesn't affect you at all.

    Improvised Weapons

    An object wielded as a makeshift weapon (broken glass, a table leg, etc.). A Simple/Martial weapon used contrary to its design (e.g. a ranged weapon in melee, or throwing a non-Thrown melee weapon) also counts as improvised.

    • Proficiency. No Proficiency Bonus applies.
    • Damage. 1d4 of a type the DM deems appropriate on a hit.
    • Range. If thrown: normal range 20 feet, long range 60 feet.
    • Weapon Equivalents. The DM may rule that an improvised weapon resembling a real one uses that weapon's rules.
    Indifferent [Attitude]

    At least 8 hours of downtime with at least 6 hours of sleep and no more than 2 hours of light activity. You must have at least 1 HP to start one, and must wait 16 hours after finishing one before starting another.

    Lore
    Planes

    The planes of existence that make up the multiverse, arranged in the Great Wheel cosmology.

    Inner Planes

    Elemental and energy planes — the raw substance from which the Material Plane is built.

    Elemental Plane of Air

    The Elemental Plane of Air is a boundless, weightless expanse of open sky with no ground and no horizon — a place where the only "up" and "down" are the ones a visitor brings with them. Vast cloud banks drift like continents, thunderheads boil without warning, and floating chunks of earth or ice — torn loose from other elemental planes by the plane's endless winds — hang suspended, some large enough to support small settlements. Storms of tornado-like violence sweep through at random, and creatures unaccustomed to the plane risk being swept away entirely if they aren't careful (or tethered).

    The plane is the native realm of air elementals and, more prominently, the djinn — genies of noble bearing who once ruled a magnificent floating city called the City of Jazz before it was destroyed. Djinn nobility still maintain floating palaces and pavilions anchored to the larger drifting landmasses, and they are known for both their hospitality and their pride. Other denizens include roaming air elementals, aarakocra who trade with the djinn, and the occasional stranded traveler from the Material Plane, since the Plane of Air borders the sky of many worlds and is one of the more commonly (if accidentally) visited Inner Planes.

    Mechanically and narratively, the plane is useful as a place to strand a party without normal footing, stage an aerial dungeon among floating islands, or send a spellcaster who's badly misread a plane shift or gate spell. Its portals tend to open onto windswept cliffs, mountain peaks, or storm clouds on the Material Plane — the sky is never far from this plane's influence, even for people who never intend to visit it.

    2024 rules note: like all Inner Planes, the Plane of Air carries no special environmental hazard rules by default — a DM narrates its effects (turbulence, disorientation, difficulty finding solid ground) as needed for the scene, using Difficult Terrain, Exhaustion from severe weather, or ability checks as fits.

    Elemental Plane of Earth

    The Elemental Plane of Earth is an infinite mass of solid stone, packed soil, and mineral veins, riddled throughout with caverns, tunnels, and pockets of open air that its native inhabitants treat the way surface dwellers treat rooms in a house. There is no sky here, only rock in every direction; light comes from veins of luminous minerals, pockets of magma, or whatever a visitor brings. Movement through solid stone is entirely natural for creatures native to the plane, who part the earth around them as they go.

    Its dominant inhabitants are the dao, genies of covetous, greedy temperament who carve out sprawling underground kingdoms rich with gemstones and precious metals, ruling them the way a miserly noble rules an estate. Dao are notorious for enslaving other creatures — including elementals of their own plane — to labor in their mines, and they're shrewd, transactional negotiators who rarely give anything away for free. Alongside the dao, the plane is home to earth elementals, galeb duhr (living boulders), and xorn, burrowing creatures that famously eat precious metals and gemstones as food.

    For a DM, the Plane of Earth is a natural source for stone golems' origins, deep mining operations gone wrong, dao patrons who offer magic in exchange for something buried, or portals that dump a party into solid rock they must dig or magic their way out of. Its connection to the Material Plane usually surfaces in deep cave systems, mines, and quarries — places where the boundary between the worlds has worn thin.

    2024 rules note: as with the other Inner Planes, treat unusual movement, breathing, or orientation challenges as DM-narrated hazards rather than a fixed rules block — earth native creatures (and anyone with Earth Glide or similar) move through it with ease; everyone else needs magic or mundane tools to manage.

    Elemental Plane of Fire

    The Elemental Plane of Fire is an endless sea of flame, molten rock, and drifting embers, its "landscape" made of solidified lava islands surrounded by oceans of fire. Heat radiates from everything; anything flammable that enters unprotected burns almost immediately, and even fire-resistant visitors find the air itself scalding to breathe. Rivers of magma flow where water would elsewhere, and volcanic eruptions of ash and flame occur with no warning.

    At its heart lies the City of Brass, the greatest fixed landmark on the plane and the seat of power of the efreet — proud, cruel genies who rule as absolute sultans over slaves and lesser fire creatures alike. The City of Brass is a wonder of towering minarets, brass domes, and bazaars where anything can be bought for the right price (often a price paid in service or freedom). Efreet nobility scheme constantly against one another and against rival genie courts, particularly the marids of the Elemental Plane of Water, with whom they share a long-standing rivalry. Fire elementals, salamanders, and azer (dour, dwarf-like smiths) round out the plane's population.

    Adventures set here often revolve around efreet bargains (which always have a catch), rescuing or bargaining with slaves of the City of Brass, or braving the fire plane's terrain to reach a forge or artifact that can only be found — or crafted — amid its flames. Its connection to the Material Plane shows up most often in volcanoes, active forges, and other places of extreme heat.

    2024 rules note: exposure to the plane's native heat and flame is a DM-adjudicated environmental hazard (fire damage, Exhaustion, or similar) rather than a codified rule — creatures with fire immunity or resistance fare best, and most visitors need protective magic to survive for long.

    Elemental Plane of Water

    The Elemental Plane of Water is a boundless ocean without a surface or a floor, its depths shading gradually from sunlit-seeming shallows near its "surface" (such as it is) to crushing, lightless trenches far below. Currents replace wind, islands of coral and stone drift like clouds, and enormous aquatic megafauna patrol territories the size of small seas. Visitors without magical or natural means of breathing water and moving through it are at the mercy of the plane almost immediately.

    The marids, most regal and least overtly cruel of the elemental genie courts, rule sprawling coral palaces and sunken cities from thrones of pearl and abyssal stone. Though proud, marids tend toward hospitality rather than tyranny (at least by genie standards), and they're often approached by mortals seeking knowledge of the sea or safe passage through it. Sharing the depths are water elementals, aboleths lurking in the deepest trenches, and sahuagin who raid the plane's edges from the Material Plane. Long-standing enmity between the marids and the efreet of the Plane of Fire occasionally spills into open conflict along the border where the two Inner Planes brush against each other.

    For adventures, this plane fits underwater dungeons, lost civilizations of aquatic humanoids, marid bargains for sea-related magic, or a shipwrecked party trying to survive an ocean with no shore to find. Its Material Plane connections appear in deep ocean trenches, whirlpools, and other places where vast bodies of water meet.

    2024 rules note: as with the other Inner Planes, breathing, pressure, and currents are DM-narrated hazards — creatures with a swim speed, water breathing, or similar adaptations manage far better than those without.

    Positive Energy Plane

    The Positive Energy Plane is not so much a place as a source — an overwhelming, blinding radiance of pure life-giving energy that has no landscape, no landmarks, and (by most accounts) no fixed inhabitants of its own. Creatures that enter unprotected are quickly overwhelmed: flesh grows and swells uncontrollably, wounds knit shut in an instant, and a creature can be killed by simply being given too much life at once, its body unable to contain the energy pouring into it. Even light itself is "too much" here — most visitors need magical protection just to perceive anything besides an all-consuming glow.

    Rather than being explored, the Positive Energy Plane is usually tapped: it is the wellspring that powers healing magic throughout the multiverse, and channels of it — sometimes called positive energy conduits — connect it to other planes, including the Material Plane, feeding life into growing things and newborn creatures wherever its energy leaks through. Some divine and primal magic draws directly on this plane, and creatures native to it (or created from it, such as some celestials) are saturated with its energy in ways that make them radiant, vigorous, and resistant to death itself.

    Few adventures take place "on" the Positive Energy Plane in a traditional sense — there's nothing to walk on and nothing to fight over — but it makes an excellent source for an artifact's power, the origin of a plague of uncontrolled growth on the Material Plane, or the destination for a ritual meant to restore life on a scale normal magic can't manage.

    2024 rules note: exposure to raw positive energy is a DM-adjudicated hazard (potentially lethal without protection) rather than a fixed rules block; it's best used as a narrative location rather than a combat map.

    Negative Energy Plane

    The Negative Energy Plane is the grim mirror of its positive counterpart: a lightless, silent void that saps life and warmth from anything that enters it. There is no landscape here either — only an oppressive darkness that swallows sound and light alike, and a cold that has nothing to do with temperature. Living creatures unprotected in this place wither rapidly, their life force drawn away, while constructs and objects are unaffected and undead creatures are, by contrast, perfectly at home.

    This plane is the ultimate source of the energy that animates undead throughout the multiverse; necromantic magic that creates or empowers the undead draws, directly or indirectly, on channels leading back to this place. Some scholars and villains alike seek out points where the Negative Energy Plane bleeds through into the Material Plane, since such places make ideal sites for powerful necromantic rituals, undead-infested dungeons, or creeping zones of blight where nothing living can survive for long. A handful of things are said to dwell within the void itself — night hags are sometimes associated with it, and older lore speaks of formless things native to the darkness — but little about the plane is truly known, since few who linger there return to report it.

    As with its positive counterpart, this plane works best as a conceptual source and destination rather than a place to stage a full adventure: the origin of a lich's power, the far end of a haunted portal, or the true nature of a "cursed" region back on the Material Plane.

    2024 rules note: exposure is a DM-adjudicated hazard (potentially lethal life drain) rather than a codified rule; undead creatures and constructs are typically unaffected or even strengthened by it.

    Transitive Planes

    The connective planes travelers pass through to reach everywhere else.

    Astral Plane

    The Astral Plane is a silvery void of endless, directionless space, lit by no visible sun yet never fully dark, where distance and gravity work more like suggestions than rules. Creatures here simply will themselves to move in any direction at a speed limited only by concentration, and it is entirely possible to travel for what feels like days without any landmark in sight. Scattered through the void are astral color pools — shimmering, circular openings that serve as gateways to the Outer Planes and the Material Plane — along with the drifting corpses of dead gods, whose slowly decaying bodies (some large enough to support ecosystems of scavengers and squatters) are among the plane's few fixed landmarks.

    The Astral Plane is best known as the home of the githyanki, a martial, imperialistic people who raid across the planes from their massive fortress-cities built on the backs of astral corpses, riding red dragons and wielding silver swords capable of permanently severing a creature's silver cord — the tether that lets a traveler's spirit find its way back to its body. Losing that cord while astrally projecting is one of the plane's few universally understood dangers.

    The plane is most often reached through the astral projection spell (which sends only a creature's spirit, leaving the body behind) or through certain magic items and divine transport, and it functions as the multiverse's primary "highway" between the Material Plane and the Outer Planes — most planar travel that doesn't use a plane shift-style spell to jump directly passes through the Astral Plane at some point along the way.

    In play, the Astral Plane is a good setting for a fortress raid against githyanki war parties, an expedition to loot the corpse of a dead god, or a chase scene where the only way to lose a pursuer is to dive through the right color pool before they do.

    Ethereal Plane

    The Ethereal Plane exists as a kind of misty overlay to reality, made up of two distinct regions. The Border Ethereal is a thin, fog-shrouded layer that overlaps the Material Plane (and the Inner Planes) almost exactly — a creature there can see into the Material Plane as a ghostly, muffled, colorless image, but can't normally be seen or heard by anyone on the other side, and vice versa. The Deep Ethereal, farther "in," is a directionless mist where distance loses meaning much as it does on the Astral Plane, and where curtains of color mark transitions to the Inner Planes.

    Few creatures make the Ethereal Plane a permanent home, though ghosts, certain oozes, and a scattering of other creatures are known to linger in the Border Ethereal, sometimes able to interact weakly with the Material Plane from just beyond its edge — a phenomenon many mortals mistake for haunting. Spells like etherealness let a creature step fully into the Border Ethereal, walking through walls (from the Material Plane's perspective) and moving unseen through spaces that would otherwise be solid, since only the outlines of large structures bleed through from one side to the other.

    Because it borders the Material Plane so closely, the Ethereal Plane is a favorite tool for infiltration, escape, and surveillance — a rogue phasing through a vault wall, a ghost haunting the halls of its old home, or a wizard eavesdropping on a private meeting from just out of reach. It's less a destination in its own right than a permeable membrane wrapped around every other plane it touches.

    Material Plane & Echoes

    The “real world” and its two mirror planes.

    Material Plane

    The Material Plane is the plane of mortal life — the world (or worlds) where most D&D campaigns take place, home to ordinary physics, ordinary time, and the overwhelming majority of the multiverse's mortal population. It's the trunk from which every other plane branches, directly or indirectly: the Inner Planes provide its raw substance, the Feywild and Shadowfell echo its shape, and the Outer Planes receive the souls of its dead. Unlike most other planes, the Material Plane isn't defined by a single unifying trait or theme — it can hold anything, from freezing tundra to blazing desert to floating cities, because it's simply "the world" in the way most people understand the term.

    A single Material Plane can, and usually does, contain multiple distinct worlds and settings (Toril of the Forgotten Realms, Oerth of Greyhawk, Eberron, and countless homebrew worlds are all considered part of "the" Material Plane, existing as separate worlds within it rather than separate planes). Most spells, most monsters, and most treasure exist here by default; planar travel exists largely so that Material Plane characters have somewhere unusual to go.

    Because it's the default setting for nearly everything, the Material Plane rarely needs its own "lore entry" describing a theme or ruler the way the other planes do — its lore is whatever your campaign world's history, geography, and factions say it is. This entry mainly exists to mark its place at the center of the cosmology: the plane every other plane is defined in relation to.

    Feywild

    The Feywild is a wild, vivid echo of the Material Plane, overlapping it so closely that the two are sometimes called "coexistent" — a forest on the Material Plane might have a corresponding (but far stranger) forest in the Feywild, a lake might have a mirror lake, and a traveler can sometimes cross between the two almost without noticing, especially at particular fey crossings, standing stones, or on nights of special significance. Colors run more saturated here, emotions run closer to the surface, and the passage of time is notoriously unreliable: a night spent dancing at a fey revel can turn out, on returning to the Material Plane, to have been a year and a day.

    The Feywild is the home of the fey — a broad category of creatures including dryads, satyrs, pixies, hags, and the towering, terrifyingly beautiful archfey who rule its courts. Fey society is traditionally split between two great courts: the Seelie Court (or Court of Stars), associated with summer, generosity, and (comparatively) benevolent bargains, and the Unseelie Court (or Court of Shadow), associated with winter, cruelty, and bargains designed to trap the unwary. Both courts are dangerous in their own way — fey promises are binding in ways mortals rarely appreciate until it's too late, and fey notions of "fair" rarely line up with mortal ones.

    Adventures set in the Feywild lean into fairy-tale logic: riddles that must be answered exactly, bargains with hidden costs, revels that are lethal to leave early, and beauty that conceals something predatory. It's an excellent plane for a memorable one-shot detour or an entire campaign built around fey politics.

    Shadowfell

    The Shadowfell is the Feywild's dark twin — another echo of the Material Plane, overlapping it just as closely, but drained of color, muffled of sound, and soaked in an ambient despair that presses on visitors the longer they stay. Where the Feywild exaggerates joy and wonder, the Shadowfell exaggerates gloom and hopelessness; prolonged exposure is known to wear away at a creature's spirit, and it's a plane many adventurers are glad to leave.

    The plane is closely associated with death, undeath, and shadow magic. It's the ancestral home of shadar-kai, elves twisted by long exposure to the plane into a grim, pain-tolerant people bound in service to the Raven Queen, a powerful figure associated with death and fate whose exact status (goddess, exarch, or something else) varies between campaign settings. The Shadowfell is also home to the domains of dread — isolated demiplane pockets, each ruled by a dark lord bound to it by tragedy or sin, the most famous being Barovia, ruled by the vampire Count Strahd von Zarovich. These domains are sealed off from the rest of the Shadowfell (and from each other) by magical mists, making them ideal self-contained horror settings.

    Shadowfell settlements on the Material Plane's mirror side tend to be grim, twisted reflections of their sunlit counterparts — ruined, abandoned, or populated by the undead. It's the natural home for necromancers, haunted-house adventures, and any storyline that wants to lean into gothic horror without leaving the game's established cosmology.

    Outer Planes

    Planes of pure moral and philosophical alignment, home to gods, celestials, fiends, and the souls of the dead. Arranged around the Outlands in the Great Wheel.

    Upper Planes

    The good-aligned Outer Planes.

    Mount Celestia

    Mount Celestia is the plane of unwavering, disciplined goodness — Lawful Good made manifest as an ascending mountain whose seven layers (Lunia, Mercuria, Venya, Solania, Mertion, Jovar, and Chronias) rise from gentle, welcoming foothills to a blindingly radiant summit. Climbing the mountain is treated throughout its lore as a metaphor for spiritual ascension: the higher a soul climbs, the purer and more powerful the goodness it must embody to continue, and only the most righteous beings in existence ever reach the plane's peak.

    The plane is home to archons, a race of celestial soldiers and servants organized into a strict hierarchy of rank, each sworn to defend Mount Celestia and, by extension, the cause of Good throughout the multiverse. Powerful solars and other high-ranking celestials are said to dwell near the summit, while the lower layers host welcoming way-stations for virtuous souls arriving from the Material Plane. Despite its reputation for serenity, Mount Celestia is far from undefended — its archon legions are a match for the armies of the Nine Hells, with whom the plane has an old and bitter rivalry, since devils see the ordered, willing goodness of Celestia as an affront to their own ordered evil.

    Adventures set here tend to involve proving one's worth (literally, sometimes mechanically) to climb higher, seeking an archon patron or ally, or defending the plane's lower reaches from an incursion by devils or other evil forces looking to strike at Good on its own ground.

    Bytopia

    Bytopia is a plane of two paired realms, Dothion and Shurrock, sharing the same physical space back-to-back so that "up" on one side eventually becomes "down" on the other — travelers can, in theory, walk from one realm to the other simply by going far enough in one direction, though few bother, since crossing takes a very long time. Dothion is a realm of soft pastoral beauty — rolling hills, orchards, and quiet farmland tended with quiet contentment — while Shurrock is rockier and wilder, favored by craftsmen and laborers who find satisfaction in honest work rather than idle rest.

    Bytopia's alignment sits between Lawful Good and Neutral Good, and its inhabitants reflect that: rather than grand cosmic struggles, life here centers on craft, community, and the simple, unglamorous virtue of doing good work well. Gnomes and halflings are especially drawn to the plane, and many of its settlements are said to be idealized versions of small farming or crafting towns — the kind of place a tired soul imagines when they picture "somewhere peaceful." Even so, Bytopia isn't without danger at its edges, where the plane borders less friendly neighbors and where old grudges between rival crafting guilds or farming communities can simmer for centuries.

    As a setting, Bytopia works well for a quieter, slower adventure — a mystery in a idyllic village, a celestial artisan who needs help finishing a masterwork, or a peaceful home base between more dangerous planar excursions.

    Elysium

    Elysium is goodness in its purest, least complicated form — a plane of unadulterated joy, free of the moral tension that colors most of its neighbors. Its three layers (Amoria, Eronia, and Belierin) are each described as achingly beautiful in their own way: verdant fields, tranquil seas, and gentle wilderness respectively, all suffused with a contentment so complete that visitors sometimes find themselves reluctant to leave, or even to remember why they came.

    Because Elysium is defined by unclouded goodness rather than any particular philosophy of law or chaos, it lacks the rigid hierarchies of Mount Celestia or the wild passion of Arborea — its denizens simply are good, without needing doctrine or discipline to make them so. This makes the plane a popular afterlife destination for souls who lived simply virtuous lives without ever swearing themselves to a grand cause, and a common home for benevolent nature spirits and guardian creatures who ask for nothing in return for their help.

    The plane's greatest narrative danger is its greatest virtue: its serenity can be genuinely difficult to leave. Stories set in Elysium sometimes hinge on convincing a visitor (or a party member) that their work back on the Material Plane isn't finished yet, or on rescuing someone who wandered in and simply forgot to want to go home. It also makes an effective contrast when something evil manages to intrude on it — corruption here reads as especially horrifying precisely because the plane is otherwise so untroubled.

    The Beastlands

    The Beastlands (sometimes called Krigala after its first layer) is a wilderness plane where nature exists in an idealized, harmonious state — untamed but never cruel, red in tooth and claw only in the honest way that predator and prey have always coexisted, without the waste or malice that can taint nature on the Material Plane. Its three layers — Krigala, Brux, and Karasuthra — each represent different wild biomes, from deep forest to open plains to something closer to primordial jungle, and the plane's day and night cycle is tied to the phases of a great constellation rather than any single sun.

    Its native creatures include noble, often intelligent animal spirits, and it's considered the afterlife destination of choice for animals and for druids, rangers, and others whose devotion lay with the natural world rather than any temple or cause. Talking beasts, protective spirits, and celestial animals of great size and wisdom watch over the plane's wild places, and the border between "beast" and "person" is far less clear-cut here than it is on the Material Plane — visitors sometimes find themselves conversing with a creature they'd have hunted back home.

    The Beastlands makes a strong setting for a druid's personal questline, a hunt (in the respectful, spiritual sense) for a legendary animal guide, or a story about what an untainted, unspoiled wilderness actually looks like — a useful contrast if your campaign's Material Plane wilderness has been scarred by corruption, overhunting, or civilization's spread.

    Arborea

    Arborea (sometimes still called Olympus, especially in its first layer) is the plane of passionate, untamed goodness — Chaotic Good expressed not as gentle serenity but as fierce, overflowing feeling: fierce loyalty, fierce joy, fierce artistic expression, and, when provoked, fierce battle. Its three layers — Olympus, Aerdirn, and Arvandor — each carry their own flavor of wild beauty, from towering mountain halls to endless forest to Arvandor itself, the elven paradise and spiritual homeland of the elven people and their pantheon.

    Arborea's inhabitants live loudly: its feasting halls echo with music and celebration as often as its peaks echo with the clash of friendly (and unfriendly) combat, since a good fight is considered, by many of the plane's residents, as valid an expression of joy as a good song. This isn't a plane of restraint or hierarchy — decisions here are made by passion and conviction rather than law, which sometimes puts its inhabitants at odds even with other good-aligned planes that value order more highly. Elves who die and find their way to Arvandor are said to be reunited with their people's gods and ancestors in a realm that feels, to them, like the truest possible home.

    As a setting, Arborea suits high-energy stories: a feast that turns into a duel of honor, a bardic competition with real magical stakes, or a call to arms against something that threatens Arvandor and the elven diaspora it represents.

    Ysgard

    Ysgard is a plane built from Norse myth — a realm of mountains, fjords, and endless halls of feasting and battle, governed by the belief that a life without struggle, without risk, and without glory isn't worth living. Its three layers (Ysgard proper, fiery Muspelheim, and the dwarven forge-realm of Nidavellir) each lean into a different flavor of that ethos, from grand mountain halls to the primal fire that forged the plane's earliest weapons and wonders.

    Ysgard sits at the boundary between Chaotic Good and Chaotic Neutral, and its culture reflects both: heroism and loyalty to one's companions are prized above almost everything, but so is personal freedom and the thrill of a worthy fight for its own sake. Slain warriors who lived and died by this code are said to find their way here, joining endless feasts that are interrupted, night after night, by equally endless battles fought purely for the joy of fighting — wounds heal, the mead flows again, and the cycle repeats, an eternity of glory without true loss. Dwarven smiths of Nidavellir are counted among the finest crafters in the multiverse, forging weapons and wonders sought after even by other planes.

    Ysgard works well as a setting for a tournament arc, a raid alongside boisterous planar allies, or a quest to commission (or steal) a legendary weapon from the forges of Nidavellir — and it's an easy plane to drop a Norse-flavored subplot or NPC into without feeling out of place.

    Arcadia

    Arcadia sits at the boundary between Lawful Good and Lawful Neutral, and it shows: this is goodness expressed through order, discipline, and careful planning rather than passion or spontaneity. Its three layers — Abellio, Buxenus, and Sylvania — are described as tidy, well-organized realms of rolling farmland, orderly forests, and disciplined settlements where nothing is wasted and very little is left to chance. Even Arcadia's wilderness feels curated, as if nature itself agreed to a schedule.

    The plane's inhabitants value duty, structure, and the well-being of the community over individual glory, and its societies tend to be efficient, harmonious, and (compared to the plane's chaotic good neighbors like Arborea and Ysgard) somewhat lacking in spontaneity — a trade its residents consider well worth making. Arcadia sometimes finds itself allied with Lawful Neutral Mechanus against threats to cosmic order, despite its firm commitment to Good, since it shares more common ground with disciplined neutrality than its fellow good-aligned planes might expect.

    As a setting, Arcadia suits stories about duty and structure: a well-run celestial bureaucracy that still needs saving from something, a disciplined order of good-aligned knights or monks who could use outside help, or a contrast piece for players used to "good" always meaning "wild and free" — here, goodness looks a lot more like careful stewardship.

    Lower Planes

    The evil-aligned Outer Planes.

    The Nine Hells (Baator)

    The Nine Hells, also called Baator, is the plane of Lawful Evil — a realm of rigid, merciless hierarchy where every devil knows its place and spends eternity scheming to rise above it. Its nine layers (Avernus, Dis, Minauros, Phlegethos, Stygia, Malbolge, Maladomini, Cania, and Nessus) form a descending gauntlet of increasingly hostile, increasingly powerful territory, with each layer ruled by an archduke or archduchess who answers, at least nominally, to Asmodeus, the Lord of the Nine Hells and undisputed ruler of the entire plane.

    Devilkind's defining trait is the infernal contract: nearly every interaction a devil has with a mortal is structured as a binding bargain, carefully worded to extract far more than it appears to give, and devils take enormous, patient pleasure in watching mortals damn themselves through their own signatures rather than through force. The Nine Hells is locked in the ages-long Blood War against the demons of the Abyss — a conflict so old and so total that it shapes much of the Lower Planes' politics, with devils viewing their war against unstructured demonic chaos as proof of their own superior, disciplined evil.

    Adventures set in the Nine Hells often revolve around infernal contracts (breaking one, negotiating one, or discovering a loved one signed one), diplomatic missions between rival archdukes, or expeditions into the war-torn layer of Avernus, the plane's front line against the Abyss and the layer most often visited by outsiders.

    Gehenna

    Gehenna is a plane of treacherous volcanic mountains whose slopes are too steep to stand on safely and too sheer to climb without risking a fall into the fire below — a fitting home for the yugoloths, mercenary fiends who embody betrayal as a way of life rather than an occasional tactic. The plane sits between Neutral Evil and Lawful Evil, and its culture reflects a cold, calculating selfishness: nothing here is done out of loyalty, only out of self-interest, and even alliances between yugoloths are understood by both sides to last only as long as they're useful.

    Yugoloths are best known throughout the multiverse as the Blood War's professional soldiers-for-hire, willing to fight for devils, demons, or anyone else who can afford their price, and equally willing to switch sides mid-conflict if the money or advantage shifts. Their home plane mirrors this philosophy in its very geography: Gehenna offers no safe footing, no stable alliance, and no reward without a constant, exhausting vigilance against betrayal from every direction, including one's own allies of convenience.

    As a setting, Gehenna suits stories about mercenary contracts, fiendish double-crosses, or a party navigating a deal with yugoloths who are technically neutral in the Blood War but never, ever trustworthy — useful when you want an "evil but professional" antagonist who won't simply attack on sight.

    Hades (The Gray Waste)

    Hades, often called the Gray Waste, is a plane of Neutral Evil despair — an endless, colorless wasteland under a sky the same lifeless gray as the ground, where hope itself seems to drain out of visitors the longer they stay, much like the Shadowfell's gloom but far more absolute and far more dangerous. Its three layers — Oinos, Niflheim, and Pluton — offer variations on the same bleak theme: barren plains, frozen wastes, and ashen ruin.

    The plane is infamous for the larvae that squirm through its soil — the wretched, worm-like forms taken by many souls judged evil but not evil enough (or not devoted enough) to earn a "proper" fiendish existence elsewhere. These larvae are harvested by devils, demons, and yugoloths alike as raw material to be twisted into lesser fiends, making Hades a kind of grim resource ground fought over by all three fiendish factions even though none of them consider it home. Petitioners and wandering spirits here are said to slowly lose their memories and sense of self, worn down by the plane's crushing despair until almost nothing remains of who they were.

    Hades works well as a setting for a rescue mission (finding a soul before it degrades past saving), a grim harvesting operation run by rival fiends that the party stumbles into, or simply as an oppressive, sanity-testing backdrop for a journey to somewhere worse.

    Carceri

    Carceri is a prison of a plane — its very name evokes incarceration, and its lore leans into that fully: this is where beings who betrayed their own masters, broke sacred oaths, or otherwise proved too dangerous to leave free are bound, sometimes for eternity. Its six layers grow progressively colder and more desolate, from the icy wastes of its outer layers down to Agathys, its frigid, black-iced bottommost layer, and every layer is designed, in one way or another, to make escape feel tantalizingly close and permanently out of reach.

    The plane's dominant native inhabitants, demodands (also called farastu), are themselves former demons twisted and exiled here as punishment, giving Carceri's entire ecosystem a theme of failure, betrayal, and consequence. Every prisoner here — mortal or fiend — is nursing some old grievance or broken oath, and the plane's atmosphere of paranoid entrapment tends to turn even temporary alliances toxic almost immediately, since everyone assumes (often correctly) that everyone else is looking for a way to use them to escape.

    As a setting, Carceri is built for exactly one kind of story: the prison break, whether the party is trying to free someone bound here, retrieve something (or someone) dangerous that was locked away for good reason, or — worse — realize partway through that they're the ones who aren't meant to leave.

    The Abyss

    The Abyss is Chaotic Evil given form — not a plane with a fixed number of layers so much as an effectively infinite, ever-shifting labyrinth of hostile, nightmarish landscapes, each one shaped (and sometimes reshaped entirely) by the will of whichever demon lord currently claims it as territory. Where the Nine Hells is defined by order, the Abyss is defined by its absence: demons war constantly against each other as much as against anyone else, loyalty is essentially nonexistent, and even the plane's geography can't be trusted to stay the same from one visit to the next.

    Demon lords such as Demogorgon, Orcus, Graz'zt, and Baphomet (among many others) rule their own layers like personal fiefdoms, each embodying a different specific horror — madness, undeath, temptation, and savage bestial slaughter, respectively, to name just a few — and their endless struggles against one another are as much a part of the Abyss's nature as its landscape. The plane's demons are locked in the eternal Blood War against the devils of the Nine Hells, though unlike the devils' disciplined campaigns, demonic "strategy" often amounts to little more than overwhelming numbers and gleeful destruction.

    Adventures in the Abyss tend to be high-stakes and high-horror: infiltrating a demon lord's layer, surviving a plane where the ground itself might turn against you, or confronting the source of a demonic incursion that's spilled over into the Material Plane. It's best used sparingly, as a deep-end destination rather than a casual excursion.

    Pandemonium

    Pandemonium is a plane of solid rock riddled with an endless maze of narrow, twisting tunnels through which a constant, howling wind never stops blowing — a wind that carries with it a low, ceaseless moan said to slowly erode the sanity of anyone who stays too long. There is no true open sky here, only tunnel after tunnel, and its four layers offer little variation beyond degrees of narrowness, darkness, and madness-inducing noise.

    Sitting between Chaotic Neutral and Chaotic Evil, Pandemonium isn't ruled by any single overarching authority the way the Nine Hells or even the Abyss are — its native inhabitants (scattered fiends, madmen, and creatures that have simply given in to the plane's whispering wind) tend toward isolated, paranoid existences rather than organized societies, since Pandemonium's constant psychological pressure makes lasting cooperation almost impossible. Some who arrive sane don't stay that way, and legends throughout the multiverse use exile "to Pandemonium" as a particular kind of threat, distinct from being cast into the Abyss or the Nine Hells — a punishment less about suffering and more about slowly, quietly losing yourself.

    As a setting, Pandemonium suits a claustrophobic dungeon crawl through narrow, wind-scoured tunnels, a horror story about a party member's grip on reality slipping the longer they stay, or a hunt for someone who fled here specifically because they knew almost no one would follow.

    Acheron

    Acheron is a battlefield plane given permanent, physical form — its landscape made up of colossal cubes of iron and rock, some the size of small moons, that drift and slowly collide with one another across an infinite black void, each impact capable of leveling anything unlucky enough to be caught on the surface. Its three named regions — Avalas, Thuldanin, and Ocanthus — describe different sizes and densities of these drifting cubes rather than truly separate layers.

    Sitting between Lawful Evil and Lawful Neutral, Acheron is the plane where war itself is treated almost as a philosophy — armies here fight not always out of hatred but sometimes out of pure, disciplined martial commitment, entire legions locked in campaigns that have been grinding on, cube against cube, for longer than most of their soldiers can remember. Some of these armies are mortal exiles or summoned conscripts; others are native fiends and constructs bred or built for nothing but war. Rusted battlefields, wrecked fortresses, and the bones of soldiers from a thousand forgotten campaigns litter every cube's surface, a permanent record of just how long the fighting has gone on.

    As a setting, Acheron works well for a mercenary company subplot, a doomed legion the party can choose to aid or oppose, or the discovery that a Material Plane war has an eerie, deliberate mirror being fought out here — one that someone back home might be using for their own ends.

    Neutral & Other Outer Planes

    Outer Planes that don't fall neatly on the good/evil axis — the hub of the wheel and the planes of pure order and pure chaos.

    The Outlands & Sigil

    The Outlands is the plane of true, unwavering Neutrality, and it occupies a unique position in the Great Wheel: it forms the central hub from which all sixteen Outer Planes radiate outward like spokes, connected to the Outlands by gate-towns — border settlements, each thematically tied to the plane it borders, where travelers from that plane and the Outlands mingle (often uneasily). The Outlands itself is a plane of vast, quiet plains stretching toward a horizon that always seems slightly too far away, its landscape shifting subtly in tone the closer one gets to any particular gate-town's neighboring plane.

    Rising from the very center of the Outlands is the Spire, an impossibly tall mountain whose peak is claimed to be infinitely distant and unreachable by any known means. Atop (or perhaps around) the Spire floats Sigil, the City of Doors — a ring-shaped metropolis, torus-curved so its streets loop back on themselves, containing an uncountable number of magical portals that connect, in theory, to anywhere in the multiverse. Sigil is ruled with silent, absolute authority by the Lady of Pain, an enigmatic figure who never speaks, tolerates no gods within her city, and is said to flay alive (literally) anyone foolish enough to draw her direct attention. Because of its portals, Sigil functions as the multiverse's ultimate crossroads city — home to every race, faction, and philosophy imaginable, all coexisting under an uneasy truce enforced by fear of their unknowable ruler.

    As a setting, the Outlands and Sigil work as the natural "home base" for a planar-hopping campaign — a city where any door might lead anywhere, and a neutral hub where factions from every aligned plane can plausibly cross paths without immediately going to war.

    Mechanus

    Mechanus is Lawful Neutral order taken to its most literal, physical extreme: an infinite mechanism of interlocking gears the size of continents, each turning at its own precise, unwavering speed, forming a vast, silent clockwork landscape with no true ground beyond the gears themselves. Nothing on Mechanus happens by accident or impulse — every event, however small, fits into some larger pattern of cause and effect that the plane's inhabitants understand (or are working to understand) far better than most outsiders ever will.

    The plane is home to the modrons, a race of geometrically precise constructs organized into an absolute hierarchy, from simple, one-dimensional monodrones at the bottom up through increasingly complex ranks to the Primus, the plane's supreme ruler, at the top. Modron society is famous for the Great Modron March, a mysterious, cyclical event occurring roughly once every three centuries in which vast numbers of modrons pour out of Mechanus and march in an unbroken line across the planes for exactly one year, following orders even they don't fully understand — a phenomenon that has become the source of countless legends and adventure hooks throughout the multiverse.

    As a setting, Mechanus suits stories about rigid bureaucracy taken to a cosmic scale, a broken modron (or a modron experiencing something like free will) as an unusual ally, or a Great Modron March event sweeping through a region the party cares about, whether they want it to or not.

    Limbo

    Limbo is Chaotic Neutral in its rawest form — a plane of entirely unshaped, ever-shifting matter and energy, where solid ground, liquid seas, and open air all exist only as long as someone actively wills them into being. The instant that concentration lapses, whatever a visitor shaped reverts to swirling, primordial chaos, meaning that unprepared travelers can find the very floor beneath them ceasing to exist without warning.

    Despite this instability, Limbo is home to the githzerai, an ascetic, disciplined people (distant, hostile relatives of the githyanki of the Astral Plane) who have learned to impose order on the plane's chaos through sheer force of will and meditation, building fortress-monasteries that persist only because their inhabitants never stop concentrating on keeping them real. Githzerai philosophy holds that true freedom comes from mastering chaos rather than being ruled by it — a worldview shaped directly by their plane's nature, and one that puts them in a longstanding, bitter rivalry with the githyanki, whose imperial ambitions they consider a betrayal of their shared origins as escaped slaves of the illithids.

    As a setting, Limbo works well for a mental-discipline challenge (literally shaping the environment through willpower), a githzerai monastery seeking outside help against githyanki raiders, or a disorienting, reality-bending sequence where the party can't fully trust the ground they're standing on.

    Creature Types

    The fourteen creature types used to classify every monster in the game.

    Aberration

    Aberrations are creatures whose very existence feels wrong to the natural order — beings so alien in body, mind, or origin that they unsettle other creatures on an instinctive level, regardless of whether they're actually hostile. Many aberrations hail from the Far Realm, a plane of existence so utterly foreign to normal reality that contact with it (or with creatures native to it) can warp minds and bodies alike; others are simply the products of forgotten, terrible magic, ancient experiments, or evolutionary paths that never should have worked.

    Mind flayers (illithids) are the archetypal aberration: an alien, tentacle-faced species that enslaves other minds and devours brains, ruling subterranean empires built on psionics and terror. Beholders, floating orbs bristling with eye stalks each capable of a different devastating magical effect, rule their own domains through paranoia and force, convinced (often correctly, given how many beholders exist) that every other beholder they meet is a rival to be destroyed. Other notable aberrations include gibbering mouthers (shapeless masses of eyes and mouths), aboleths (ancient, telepathic tyrants of the deep who remember every era they've lived through), and grells (floating, tentacled predators).

    What ties the type together isn't appearance or habitat but wrongness: aberrations tend to defy the normal categories other creatures fit into, often possess potent psionic or mind-altering abilities, and typically pursue goals — when they have comprehensible goals at all — that are difficult or impossible for other creatures to relate to. They make excellent villains for horror-tinged adventures, since the terror they inspire comes as much from what they represent (an uncaring, incomprehensible universe) as from any direct threat they pose.

    Beast

    Beasts are non-humanoid, ordinarily non-magical animals: the wolves, bears, horses, eagles, and other creatures that populate the natural world of the Material Plane without any innate supernatural ability of their own. Most beasts lack the capacity for complex language or abstract reasoning, instead acting on instinct, training, or simple animal intelligence, though some — like giant owls or the companions bonded to rangers and druids — are noted as unusually smart for their kind.

    The beast type also covers many "giant" or "dire" versions of ordinary animals (giant spiders, giant crabs, dire wolves) that are larger and more dangerous than their mundane counterparts but still fundamentally natural creatures rather than magical ones — the size and ferocity are treated as within nature's normal range for that world, even if extreme. Prehistoric animals like the tyrannosaurus and triceratops also fall under this type, treated as simply another (if impressively large) branch of the animal kingdom rather than anything supernatural.

    Beasts are central to several class features and spells: rangers and druids interact with them constantly through Wild Shape, animal companions, and spells like animal friendship or speak with animals, and creature-type-specific spells and abilities (such as many effects that specifically target or transform into beasts) key directly off this tag. Because they're driven by instinct rather than malice, beasts also make for morally simple combat encounters — a hungry wolf pack isn't evil, it's just hungry — which can be a useful contrast against more calculating or genuinely wicked opponents in the same adventure.

    Celestial

    Celestials are creatures native to the Upper Planes, typically created to serve the forces of good and often bound, directly or indirectly, to the will of a deity or a righteous cause. They range enormously in power and form: from small, personable guardian spirits sent to protect a single mortal, up through disciplined archon soldiers, majestic pegasi and unicorns, and finally to solars and other god-like beings whose mere presence can be enough to end a battle before it starts.

    Most celestials share a handful of traits regardless of rank: they tend to radiate an aura of goodness that other creatures can sense, many can heal or protect the innocent as easily as they fight, and a great many are immune or highly resistant to disease and to the kind of corrupting influence that would twist a lesser creature toward evil. That said, "celestial" describes an origin and general alignment tendency rather than a guarantee of perfection — some celestials fall, corrupted by the very evils they were meant to fight, becoming some of the most tragic and dangerous villains in the game's lore.

    Celestials serve a wide range of narrative roles: divine messengers delivering a quest, guardian spirits bound to protect a holy site, angelic warriors defending Mount Celestia from an infernal incursion, or a fallen celestial whose redemption (or final defeat) becomes the emotional core of an entire campaign arc. Many spellcasting classes, particularly clerics and paladins, have features and spells that summon, communicate with, or draw power from celestials directly.

    Construct

    Constructs are creatures given a semblance of life through magic, craft, or both, rather than through natural birth or growth — animated objects, golems, homunculi, and similar beings built rather than born. Because they're created rather than grown, constructs typically lack the biological needs of living creatures: many don't need to breathe, eat, sleep, or age, and a great many are immune to poison and disease entirely, along with a range of effects that specifically target a living mind or body.

    The type covers an enormous range of scale and sophistication. At the simple end sit animated objects — brooms, suits of armor, or furniture brought briefly to life by a spell — often mindless and following only the simplest instructions. Golems represent a much larger, more permanent investment: clay, stone, iron, or flesh golems are built through lengthy, costly rituals to serve as tireless guardians or laborers, generally obeying their creator's last standing orders with a literal-mindedness that can cause serious problems if those orders weren't phrased carefully. More sophisticated constructs, like warforged (a full playable species in many settings) or certain modrons of Mechanus, possess genuine independent thought and personality despite their manufactured origins.

    Constructs make excellent "safe" combat encounters when a DM wants raw physical danger without moral complexity (a mindless golem guardian isn't evil, it's just following orders), and just as easily support far more nuanced stories when a construct's growing self-awareness, free will, or personhood becomes a central question of the plot.

    Dragon

    Dragons are ancient, immensely powerful reptilian creatures, almost all capable of flight, most possessing a devastating breath weapon, and virtually all defined by a fierce, often obsessive hoarding instinct — a true dragon's lair is rarely complete without a treasure hoard the dragon knows down to the last coin. Dragons grow continuously more powerful throughout their extraordinarily long lives, passing through a series of age categories from wyrmling up through ancient (and, in some settings, beyond), each stage bringing greater size, stronger breath weapons, and more formidable magic.

    The type traditionally splits into two great families defined by color and alignment. Chromatic dragons — black, blue, green, red, and white — are usually evil, proud, and possessive, each color tied to a particular breath weapon, terrain, and temperament (reds are wrathful and covet fire and gold above all else; whites are feral and least intelligent of the chromatics; and so on). Metallic dragons — brass, bronze, copper, gold, and silver — are usually good, often taking an active interest in protecting the mortals living near their domains, though a metallic dragon's idea of "help" can still be intimidating or come with its own strings attached. Other dragon-kin, including gem dragons and a growing roster of unusual draconic species across various settings, expand the type further.

    A single dragon, appropriately built up, can serve as the climactic threat of an entire campaign — dragons in 5e lore are rarely just monsters to be fought, but individuals with real politics, ambitions, and relationships to the world around their lair.

    Elemental

    Elementals are creatures formed from the raw substance of the Inner Planes — air, earth, fire, and water — given shape, purpose, and (often but not always) a rudimentary intelligence. Most elementals native to the Material Plane arrive there through summoning magic rather than natural migration, called forth temporarily by spells like conjure elemental or, more permanently, bound into service by more elaborate rituals.

    The type includes both the classic elementals of air, earth, fire, and water — each embodying its home plane's core characteristics, from the whirling fury of an air elemental to the crushing weight of an earth elemental — and the genies who rule those same Inner Planes: djinn of air, dao of earth, efreet of fire, and marids of water. Genies occupy an interesting niche within the elemental type, since unlike simpler elementals they possess complex societies, individual personalities, and often genuine magical or wish-granting power, making them far more dangerous (and far more interesting) to bargain with than a mindless whirlwind or wall of stone.

    Elementals make natural "hired muscle" for spellcasters (a wizard summoning a fire elemental to guard a vault, for instance), and genies in particular are a reliable source of "be careful what you wish for" storytelling — a bound efreet granting wishes with a cruel technicality, or a marid demanding an impossible favor in exchange for safe passage through its waters, are classic uses of the type that lean into its planar, larger-than-life nature.

    Fey

    Fey are creatures tied closely to primal nature and to the Feywild, ranging from small, mischievous nature spirits to powerful, ancient archfey who rule entire fey courts. What unites the type isn't size or power but temperament: fey tend to operate on a logic of their own — one built around bargains, riddles, tricks, and obligations rather than straightforward force — and even well-meaning fey can be genuinely dangerous to deal with if a mortal doesn't understand the rules they're playing by.

    Smaller fey like pixies, sprites, and satyrs tend to be playful and mercurial, more interested in games, music, and mischief than in grand schemes, though their pranks can carry real consequences for anyone who takes them too lightly. Dryads and similar spirits are bound closely to a specific tree, grove, or natural feature, fiercely protective of it and largely indifferent to anything happening outside their domain. Hags represent the type's darker edge: ancient, predatory fey who trade in secrets, curses, and stolen years, often working together in covens to magnify their considerable power. At the top of fey society sit the archfey, beings of godlike power who rule the courts of the Feywild and whose favor (or wrath) can reshape a mortal's fate entirely.

    Fey work well as patrons for warlocks, as the architects of a fairy-tale-flavored side quest full of binding bargains and literal interpretations of spoken promises, or as recurring antagonists whose motives only make sense once a party learns to think the way the fey do.

    Fiend

    Fiends are creatures of the Lower Planes, given shape by evil itself and, almost without exception, actively working to spread corruption, suffering, and destruction throughout the multiverse. The type is dominated by two great, warring families: devils, the disciplined, contract-obsessed denizens of the Nine Hells, and demons, the chaotic, destruction-obsessed denizens of the Abyss, locked together in the eternal Blood War that has raged for as long as either side can remember.

    Devils operate through temptation, patience, and carefully worded bargains — a devil would usually rather trick a mortal into damning themselves than simply attack them outright, since a soul willingly given is worth far more than one merely taken by force. Demons, by contrast, tend toward direct savagery, embodying specific primal horrors (madness, plague, slaughter, corruption) with little interest in subtlety. Yugoloths of Gehenna occupy a third, mercenary niche within the type, selling their considerable martial skill to whichever side of the Blood War (or whichever mortal patron) currently pays best, loyal to nothing but their own advantage.

    Fiends make reliable, mechanically and morally uncomplicated villains — few players will feel conflicted about killing a devil or demon — while also offering rich material for stories about temptation, corruption, and the cost of a bargain made in desperation. A fiend patron for a warlock, an infernal contract gone wrong, or a demonic incursion threatening the Material Plane are all well-worn but reliably effective campaign hooks.

    Giant

    Giants are a family of towering humanoid-shaped creatures, almost always vastly larger and stronger than ordinary humanoids, and organized (in most settings) under the Ordning — a rigid social hierarchy that ranks the various giant kinds from greatest to least, with storm giants traditionally at the top and the lesser giant-kin near the bottom. This hierarchy carries enormous cultural weight among giants: a giant's rank in the Ordning shapes how it's expected to behave, who it's expected to defer to, and how seriously any perceived insult to that rank will be taken.

    Storm giants, wise and reclusive, dwell in remote mountain peaks or floating castles and rarely involve themselves in mortal affairs unless something has gone badly wrong with the Ordning itself. Cloud giants live equally high above the world, literally, in castles built on floating earthmotes, and are known for capricious, sometimes cruel whims. Fire giants run disciplined, almost militaristic forges and armies; frost giants favor raw, savage strength and raiding; stone giants prefer quiet isolation in deep caverns, often gentler than their reputation suggests; and hill giants sit at the bottom of the Ordning, favoring brute force and simple appetite over any of the sophistication their larger kin claim to possess.

    A single displeased giant can serve as a formidable regional threat on its own, while a full campaign built around giants often centers on the Ordning itself: a disruption to the hierarchy, a rogue giant defying its assigned place, or a mortal party caught in the middle of giant politics far larger than themselves, in every sense.

    Humanoid

    Humanoids are the multiverse's most populous and civilization- building creature type, encompassing the vast majority of playable species — humans, elves, dwarves, halflings, and dozens more — along with many classic "monster" societies such as orcs, goblinoids, gnolls, and kobolds. What defines the type isn't alignment or moral character (humanoid societies run the full spectrum from noble to monstrous, often within the same species) but rather a shared capacity for language, tool use, culture, and complex social organization roughly comparable to human civilization.

    Because the type is so broad, individual humanoid species and cultures carry the bulk of the type's actual characterization: elves are long-lived and often deeply connected to magic or nature depending on subrace and setting; dwarves prize craft, clan, and stone; orcs in many modern settings are given rich, non-monolithic cultures of their own rather than being treated as automatically evil, reflecting a broader shift in recent lore away from equating an entire humanoid species with villainy by default. Goblinoids, gnolls, kobolds, and other traditionally antagonistic humanoid peoples likewise increasingly receive nuanced treatment in official lore, with individual settings free to decide how monstrous — or how sympathetic — a given culture actually is.

    Humanoids make up the backbone of nearly every D&D setting's politics, economics, and conflict: player characters are humanoids, most NPCs are humanoids, and most "mundane" antagonists (bandits, cultists, rival adventurers, hostile tribes) draw from this type rather than from the more exotic ones.

    Monstrosity

    Monstrosities are a catch-all type for creatures that don't fit neatly anywhere else — frightening, often magically influenced beasts and hybrid creatures that are more dangerous, more unnatural, or simply stranger than an ordinary beast would be, without qualifying as aberrations, fiends, or any of the game's more tightly defined types. In practice, this makes monstrosity the type most closely associated with classic "monster of the week" creatures drawn from myth and legend.

    Owlbears, chimeras, manticores, medusae, and minotaurs are all classic monstrosities — creatures that often combine traits of multiple animals (or animal and humanoid traits) into something that couldn't occur through ordinary natural processes, frequently hinting at ancient magical experimentation, divine punishment, or some other unnatural origin even when the specific cause has been lost to legend. Displacer beasts, with their unsettling light-bending camouflage, and yetis, tied to remote, forbidding wilderness, likewise fall under this heading, alongside countless other regional or setting-specific monsters that a DM might invent for a single adventure without needing to invent an entirely new type to classify them.

    Because the type is intentionally broad, monstrosities work well as flexible, story-driven threats: a cursed noble transformed into a monstrosity as punishment, an ancient magical experiment still guarding its creator's ruined laboratory, or simply a dangerous, memorable "boss monster" for a single memorable encounter without needing to tie it into any larger cosmological faction.

    Ooze

    Oozes are amorphous, often mindless creatures defined by their lack of fixed shape and, in many cases, by a corrosive or otherwise hazardous touch that makes them dangerous out of proportion to their simple-seeming nature. Most oozes have little to no capacity for reasoning, instead drifting or oozing toward stimuli (warmth, movement, food) on something closer to pure instinct than intelligence, which makes them unpredictable hazards rather than calculating villains.

    Gelatinous cubes are the type's most iconic member: a perfectly transparent, room-filling cube of acidic jelly that drifts slowly through dungeon corridors, engulfing and dissolving anything (and anyone) unfortunate enough not to notice it in time — a monster whose main danger often comes from being genuinely hard to spot rather than from any cunning on its part. Black puddings, ochre jellies, and gray oozes round out the type's classic roster, each differing mainly in their exact corrosive effect, size, and (in the case of puddings and jellies) their unpleasant tendency to split into smaller copies of themselves when struck with the wrong kind of damage.

    Because oozes are rarely intelligent or motivated by anything beyond instinct, they work best as environmental hazards and dungeon-crawl set pieces rather than as characters in their own right — a gelatinous cube patrolling a corridor, an ochre jelly nesting in a flooded cellar, or a splitting pudding that turns a single fight into an escalating crowd-control problem if the party doesn't adjust their tactics.

    Plant

    Plants are creatures with a fundamentally vegetal nature — living flora given mobility, awareness, or aggression well beyond what an ordinary tree or shrub is capable of, usually through some combination of ancient magic, extraplanar influence, or simply a strange corner of nature that evolved (or was cultivated) into something dangerous. Some plant creatures retain clear traces of their mundane origins, while others barely resemble any natural plant at all beyond a loose biological theme.

    Shambling mounds are among the type's best-known members: hulking, vaguely humanoid masses of animated vegetation and rot that lurk in swamps and dense forests, strong enough to grapple and smother prey far larger than themselves. Awakened trees and shrubs — ordinary plants given sentience and mobility, usually through a druid's magic — also fall under this type, and range from helpful, thoughtful allies to bitter, territorial guardians depending on how (and by whom) they were awakened. Myconids, a society-building fungal humanoid-adjacent species with their own peaceful, spore-based culture, and treants, ancient and powerful guardians of old-growth forests, further expand the type's range from mindless menace to genuine, complex civilization.

    Plants work well as guardians of natural or sacred sites, as unsettling threats that don't fit the usual "monster" mold (a forest that's slowly, patiently hostile rather than aggressively so), or as unlikely allies for druid- and nature-themed campaigns, standing in stark contrast to more conventionally "monstrous" opposition.

    Undead

    Undead are creatures animated by negative energy or necromantic magic after death, existing in a state that mocks or perverts natural life rather than continuing it — a broad type spanning everything from mindless, shambling corpses to some of the most powerful and cunning villains in the entire game. Nearly all undead share a handful of common traits: they typically don't need to breathe, eat, or sleep, many are immune to poison and to effects that specifically target the living, and most radiate an unnatural chill or wrongness that unsettles nearby living creatures on an instinctive level.

    At the type's simpler end sit zombies and skeletons — mindless, slow-thinking undead animated to follow simple commands, more dangerous in numbers than individually. Ghosts and specters represent undeath's more tragic side: spirits bound to the Material Plane by unfinished business, unresolved trauma, or simple refusal to accept their own death, sometimes redeemable if a party can address whatever's keeping them anchored. At the type's most fearsome sit liches and vampires: liches, once-mortal spellcasters who traded their humanity and a piece of their soul (kept safe in a phylactery) for functional immortality and continued magical power; and vampires, cursed, charismatic predators who feed on the living and command lesser undead of their own, most famously exemplified by Count Strahd von Zarovich of Barovia.

    Undead serve as some of the genre's most flexible antagonists — equally at home as a mindless dungeon hazard, a tragic figure to be freed rather than destroyed, or a scheming, centuries-old villain patient enough to out-plan an entire party.

    Spell Schools

    The eight schools of magic that every spell belongs to.

    Abjuration

    Abjuration is the school of protection, warding, and denial — spells that block, banish, dispel, or shield rather than spells that create, destroy, or deceive. An abjuration spell's defining trait is that it works against something: against harm (as with shield or protection from energy), against hostile magic itself (as with counterspell and dispel magic), against a specific creature's presence in a space (as with banishment), or against intrusion and detection (as with alarm or private sanctum).

    Narratively, abjuration is strongly associated with careful, defensive spellcasters — wizards who specialize in wards and protective circles, clerics guarding a temple, or any caster whose instinct in a fight is to shore up their side rather than tear down the enemy's. The school also covers the magic behind warded strongholds, sealed vaults, and protective circles used to contain dangerous creatures or artifacts, making abjuration spells and abjuration specialists a natural fit for guardians, jailers, and anyone whose job is keeping something in (or out).

    Because abjuration spells so often directly counter other magic — dispelling curses, blocking scrying, or shutting down an enemy spellcaster's counterspell — an abjuration specialist can be a particularly satisfying, tactical kind of NPC ally or villain: not the flashiest combatant in the room, but often the one whose absence (or presence) decides whether the rest of the plan actually works.

    Conjuration

    Conjuration is the school of transportation and summoning — spells that bring something (or someone) from one place to another, whether that's a creature from another plane, an object from storage, or the caster themselves across a battlefield. This covers both summoning magic proper (conjure animals, conjure elemental, spells that call allies from elsewhere to fight alongside the caster) and teleportation magic (misty step, dimension door, teleport), along with spells that conjure solid or hazardous matter into being, such as conjuring a wall of thorns or a cloud of choking gas from nothing at all.

    Because summoning and teleportation are such distinct-feeling effects, conjuration covers a wider practical range than most other schools — a conjurer might specialize entirely in calling monstrous allies to the battlefield, entirely in blinking around a fight faster than anyone can pin them down, or in both at once. The school is also closely tied to planar magic more broadly: many of the most significant conjuration spells (plane shift, gate) are exactly the kind of spells that open the door to everything covered elsewhere in this Lore section, from Inner Planes to Outer.

    Conjurers make excellent battlefield controllers and excellent plot devices in equal measure — a villain's conjuration magic can just as easily summon a monstrous ally mid-fight as open the very portal the whole adventure has been building toward.

    Divination

    Divination is the school of knowledge and foresight — spells that reveal information the caster couldn't otherwise have: hidden creatures and objects (see invisibility, locate object), the truth behind a lie (zone of truth), the future or the likely outcome of an action (augury, divination), or secrets held in someone else's mind (detect thoughts). Where most schools change the world, divination changes what the caster (and often their allies) know about it.

    Diviners occupy a distinct narrative niche: rather than battlefield powerhouses, they tend to be advisors, investigators, oracles, and information brokers, valued as much (or more) outside combat as within it. A skilled diviner can turn a seemingly impossible mystery into a straightforward one, which makes divination magic something DMs often have to plan around carefully — a well-placed detect magic or scrying spell can short-circuit an entire investigation-based adventure if its limits (range, duration, the chance of being deceived by countermagic) aren't kept firmly in mind.

    Divination's association with fate and foresight also gives it unusually strong thematic weight: prophecy, fate, and the question of whether the future can truly be changed once glimpsed are all classic story threads that a divination-focused NPC, faction, or artifact can carry particularly well.

    Enchantment

    Enchantment is the school of mental influence — spells that affect a creature's mind directly, altering emotions, clouding judgment, or overriding will entirely. This ranges from relatively gentle effects, like calming a hostile crowd or making a suspicious guard friendlier (charm person, calm emotions), up through spells that seize outright control of another creature's actions (dominate person, dominate monster) or implant a false memory or command so deeply the target can't tell it was ever placed there (modify memory, geas).

    Because enchantment magic tampers directly with free will, it carries more inherent moral weight than most other schools — even a "helpful" use of charm magic raises uncomfortable questions about consent, and player characters who lean heavily on enchantment spells often find their allies (and their DMs) treating that choice as a meaningful character trait rather than a neutral tactical option. Fittingly, enchantment is a favorite school of manipulative villains: cult leaders who charm their followers into loyalty, hags who bind mortals with subtly worded compulsions, and fiends who'd always rather talk a mortal into damning themselves than force the issue.

    Enchantment specialists make compelling social-encounter threats precisely because their danger isn't obvious in a fight — the real damage may already be done by the time anyone realizes an NPC's "friendliness" was never entirely their own.

    Evocation

    Evocation is the school of raw elemental and magical energy — spells that manifest fire, lightning, force, radiance, or other destructive (or occasionally protective) energy directly, rather than manipulating an existing object or creature. It's the school most associated with blunt magical firepower: fireball, lightning bolt, magic missile, and scorching ray are all evocation spells, and evocation as a whole tends to be the school new players reach for first when they picture "what a wizard does" in combat.

    Not every evocation spell is destructive — light and a handful of other spells channel energy toward gentler ends — but the school's reputation, in-world and at the table alike, is overwhelmingly tied to direct, often devastating offensive magic. Evokers as specialists tend to be the most straightforwardly aggressive kind of spellcaster, favoring large, area-effect destruction over the subtler approaches other schools offer, and evocation magic is a natural fit for any battle-focused mage, whether a heroic battlemage clearing a path through enemies or a villain whose solution to every problem is simply more firepower.

    Because evocation spells are usually the most visually and mechanically dramatic option in a caster's kit, they tend to define a spellcaster's combat identity even when that caster also knows spells from several other schools — the fireball is what people remember.

    Illusion

    Illusion is the school of deception through the senses — spells that create false images, sounds, or other sensory impressions, or that hide something real behind a false one, rather than changing anything about physical reality itself. This spans everything from a simple visual trick (minor illusion) up through fully immersive, multi-sensory fabrications capable of fooling a creature's mind into believing something is actually there (major image, programmed illusion), as well as spells that conceal rather than fabricate, hiding a creature or object from detection entirely (invisibility, nondetection).

    Illusionists occupy a very different combat and roleplay niche than evokers or enchanters: rather than raw force or direct mental control, illusion magic wins fights and solves problems through misdirection, distraction, and outright trickery, rewarding creative use far more than brute-force application. A skilled illusionist can talk (or trick) their way past an entire guard post that a more direct approach would have to fight through, and illusion-based villains are a favorite for mystery and betrayal plots, since the audience — and often the party — genuinely can't trust what they're seeing until the illusion is dispelled or seen through.

    Because illusion magic depends so heavily on a viewer believing what they perceive, it interacts constantly and interestingly with other magic (particularly true seeing and other illusion-piercing effects), making illusionists satisfying recurring antagonists whose tricks a clever party can learn to anticipate over time.

    Necromancy

    Necromancy is the school of death, undeath, and the manipulation of life force — spells that harm by directly draining vitality (chill touch, vampiric touch), that raise or command the dead (animate dead, create undead), or that manipulate the line between life and death more directly still (revivify, raise dead, and their darker counterparts). Necromancy has one of the strongest, most consistent reputations of any school throughout D&D's lore: even when used for unambiguously good purposes (such as the healing-adjacent revivify), it's the school most associated, culturally and narratively, with villainy.

    Necromancers who raise and command the dead sit at the dark heart of countless D&D villain archetypes, from the lich who traded away mortality for arcane power, to the cultist raising a shambling army from a graveyard, to the more sympathetic necromancer whose magic comes from grief and a refusal to let go of someone they lost. The school's deep thematic ties to the Negative Energy Plane and the Shadowfell (see the Planes section above) mean necromancy-heavy stories often naturally lead toward those same locations, whether the party is hunting down a necromancer's source of power or trying to seal off a rift the necromancer opened.

    Because necromancy carries such heavy cultural baggage in-world, even a well-intentioned necromancer character usually has to contend with other NPCs' suspicion and fear — the school's reputation is, in most settings, entirely earned.

    Transmutation

    Transmutation is the school of physical change — spells that alter the form, properties, or substance of a creature or object, rather than summoning something new, deceiving the senses, or dealing damage directly. This covers an enormous range: shapeshifting the caster or an ally into another creature entirely (polymorph, shapechange), altering a creature's physical capabilities (haste, enlarge/reduce), or transforming matter itself, from turning stone to mud (transmute rock) to reshaping flesh into stone and back (flesh to stone).

    Transmutation's flexibility makes it one of the hardest schools to pin down thematically — it's equally at home enabling a battlefield-dominating polymorph spell, quietly transforming lead into gold, or granting a party the mundane-but-vital ability to breathe water or walk through walls to solve a specific problem. Transmuters as specialists tend to reflect that same versatility, often serving as the most broadly useful kind of spellcaster in a given group rather than one defined by a single signature effect the way an evoker or necromancer might be.

    The school's deep ties to alchemy and physical transformation also make it a natural fit for the classic "mad alchemist" or "transmuter gone too far" villain archetype — a spellcaster whose fascination with reshaping flesh and matter has stopped respecting the line between improvement and monstrosity.
